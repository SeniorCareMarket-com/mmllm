## Hypothesis
Each shard's worker consolidates a non-overlapping slice of the tool-call
distribution into its NetBank. Harvest (FedAvg or distillation) then has
genuinely complementary content to combine, not 5 near-redundant generalists.

Target: post-harvest fim_eval < 5.5 OR format_validity > 0.150 — beating
round-6's headline by a non-trivial margin.

## Read on the consolidation + harvest

**Resume baseline.** Round-7 starts from the round-6 FedAvg core, so
control bpc is already at 0.326 at step 250 (vs round-6's 0.782 starting
fresh from round-2). The mechanism enters the run with Net already
carrying meaningful signal — telemetry shows L_out=0.224 and N_out=3.67
at step 250, so Local is *already* small at the resume.

**Sleep cycles fire correctly but produce small Δ_net.** Post-sleep
Δ_net = `+0.0285 / +0.0097 / +0.0098 / +0.0023` across cycles 1–4 —
well below the +0.10 watch-list target. Δ_local pinned in
[-0.0062, +0.0019], usually slightly *negative* (Local actively hurts a
small amount of routing decisions), so the sleep mechanism is genuinely
zeroing Local each time. The reason Δ_net is so much smaller than
round-6 (which peaked at +0.262) is that round-6 began with Local-mass
~3.7 to surface and round-7 began with Local-mass ~0.2 — there's just
less *differential* signal for Δ_net to capture. The mechanism is
correctly fingerprinting "what would change if Net were absent" and
that delta is genuinely small because Net's residual contribution to
the prediction is already small when Local is also tiny and the dense
trunk + sdpa carry most of the load post-FedAvg.

**Net is still growing.** N_out 3.67 → 7.72 (2.1×), gate_w_net 0.131 →
0.250 (nearly 2×). alpha_net dampens 0.50 → 0.25 (continuing the round-6
anti-overshoot trend on top of an already-dampened starting alpha).
Effective Net contribution `alpha · w_net · N_out` grows ~0.24 → 0.49
across the run.

**val_bpc target HIT.** End-of-run control bpc = 0.258 (target < 0.40),
versus round-6's per-worker average ~0.32. The shard is genuinely
specializing on its 20% slice — and the gap (0.258 vs 0.32) is the
specialization signal at the in-distribution level.

**fim_eval per-language vs round-6 individual baseline:**

| lang     | round-7 shard-3 | round-6 individual | Δ      |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------:|-------:|
| json     |  4.209          | 3.787              | +0.42  |
| generic  |  8.298          | 6.579              | +1.72  |
| python   | 12.096          | 9.401              | +2.70  |
| clojure  | 11.624          | 8.434              | +3.19  |
| OVERALL  |  **7.281**      | 5.804 (FedAvg)     | +1.48  |

Out-of-distribution (fim_eval) is uniformly worse than round-6's FedAvg
across every language — by ~0.4 for json (closest to xlam-synth) and
~3 bpc for clojure (most off-distribution). This is the textbook
specialization-as-overfit signature: in-distribution val_bpc went *down*
(0.258 vs 0.32) and out-of-distribution went *up* (7.28 vs 5.80).

**Harvest expectation.** The hypothesis was "each shard consolidates a
non-overlapping slice of the tool-call distribution into its NetBank."
For tool *names*, this isn't true — synthetic xlam balances all tools
across all shards. What shards do differ in: specific tool-call
argument values, specific user phrasings, specific call-sequences
within docs. Whether those finer-grained differences are what the
NetBank V tensors actually encode is open. Two plausible outcomes:

1. **Harvest helps.** If NetBank V is encoding contextual argument
   patterns (which slots co-occur, which strings appear in `args`),
   then shards saw non-overlapping argument samples — 5 different V's
   averaged should give a more general argument distribution than any
   single shard.
2. **Harvest is neutral.** If NetBank V is encoding tool-name routing
   and shared skeleton patterns (every shard saw every tool name in
   ~equal proportion), then 5 V's averaged are 5 copies of roughly the
   same thing and the merge benefit is small.

My read: **outcome 2 is more likely** given the tool-distribution
analysis above. The round-7 hypothesis presumed that sharding would
produce divergent specialists; the synthetic xlam corpus undercuts the
premise by distributing tool names uniformly, so the shards diverge at
a finer grain than the NetBank's storage granularity (~1024 cells × 4
blocks, with 256-dim V) probably resolves. Round-7 will likely confirm
that the merge benefit comes from the *number* of independent runs over
the same data, not from corpus diversity per se — round-6's FedAvg gain
was already what's available from this corpus, and round-7 may add a
modest fraction of further benefit.

**If round-7 harvest beats round-6's 5.804 by < 0.3 bpc**, that's
evidence for outcome 2 and the next architectural lever is corpus-side:
generate xlam variants that *actually* differ in tool emphasis (e.g.,
"shard 0 sees only `search_web/translate/get_route`, shard 1 sees only
`run_code/create_issue/...`"). Tool-class-disjoint shards would test
the divergent-specialists hypothesis without the synthetic-uniformity
confound.

**Bottom line.** Round-7 shard-3 confirms sleep cycles work on the
harvested core (Δ_local pinned, ctrl_bpc descended monotonically modulo
LR jitter), specializes well in-distribution (val_bpc 0.258 << 0.32),
generalizes worse out-of-distribution (fim_eval 7.28 > 5.80), and the
NetBank harvest will probably show modest merge benefit because the
corpus is shard-uniform at the tool-name level.
